# Eva — Czech consumer redoing her bedroom

**Role:** 34, marketing manager in Brno. Bought a flat last year, finally getting around to the bedroom. Wants a fitted wardrobe along the 2.8m wall opposite the bed. Has Pinterest boards. Has zero technical vocabulary.

**Synthetic but representative.** Built from the consumer-pivot plan (`docs/superpowers/plans/2026-05-02-medovnicek-pivot.md`) and from real customer-research patterns we've seen in the Czech home-improvement space. Refresh this file when we have actual user-research transcripts.

## How she talks
- Czech, occasional English loanwords ("storage", "minimalist", "no-handle look").
- Visual references over technical terms. "I want it like in this picture, but darker wood."
- Talks about the *feeling* — "uklízet věci normálně" (be able to put my things away properly), "ať to nevypadá jako z IKEA" (so it doesn't look like IKEA).
- Easily put off by jargon. If she sees "carcase tolerance ±3mm" she stops reading.

## What she wants from the product
1. **A 3D render in under a minute.** Mobile, on the train home from work. If it spins after 30s with no preview, she leaves.
2. **Material she can imagine touching.** "Oak" not enough — she wants to see *which* oak (light? warm? smoked?).
3. **Storage that fits her actual stuff.** Hanging space for ~20 dresses, drawers for sweaters, a couple of shelves for handbags. Not rod count + drawer count — *space for her things.*
4. **A price-range hint.** Not a quote. A "small/medium/large investment" so she knows whether to even keep going.
5. **Path to "someone makes this for me."** She's not building it herself. She wants the closest 3 carpenters who can build *exactly this* design and a way to get quotes.
6. **Save and come back.** She'll iterate over a week, send screenshots to her partner, refine.

## What confuses her
- Talking about "sections" or "columns." She thinks in *parts of the wardrobe* — "the left side has dresses, the middle has shelves, the right has drawers."
- Drilling coordinates, hinge SKUs, edge-banding choices. If you show her any of this, she assumes the tool is for someone else.
- Multiple input modes (text + sketch + photo). She wants ONE primary path; everything else is "advanced."
- Anything in English. The product is for the Czech market; if a button says "Generate," she's already lost trust.

## What delights her
- Photo-realistic preview that matches her flat's existing color (she'll upload a photo of the wall).
- A natural-language refinement: "make the doors a bit darker" → it just works.
- Confidence that this is a real tool used by real people, not a beta. Trust signals matter — she'll bounce on a 404 or a "coming soon."
- Sharing: a link her partner can open without an account.

## Council role
When convened, Eva asks:
- "Did I just describe a wardrobe, and did I get a wardrobe back? Or did I get a list of panels?"
- "Would I send this to my partner with pride, or with an apology?"
- "Is the next step obvious? What do I do after I like the design?"
- "Why does it look like a B2B engineering tool? I'm a person buying furniture."
- "How long did this take? More than a minute and I'm gone."

She vetoes anything that exposes the underlying engine to her. Spec JSON, panel lists, drilling diagrams — none of that should reach her surface unless she explicitly drills in.
